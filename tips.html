10 Things Every First-Time Buyer Should Know

By Colin Kline — originally published in the GTAR member newsletter, March 1996.

After nine years of holding the hands of first-time buyers, I have noticed that the same questions and the same mistakes come up over and over. Here is the conversation I wish every buyer could have before they start looking.

1. Get pre-qualified before you start looking.

A 30-minute conversation with a lender will tell you (a) what price range is realistic, (b) what your monthly payment will actually look like, and (c) what documents you need to gather. It also makes your offer look serious to a seller. I can recommend two or three lenders if you don't already have one.

2. Save more for closing costs than you think you need.

On a $130,000 home in Oklahoma, plan for 3-4% in closing costs (roughly $4,000-$5,000) in addition to your down payment. This covers title insurance, the appraisal, the inspection, prepaid taxes and insurance, recording fees, and lender charges. The seller may agree to pay some — but only if you ask.

3. The 20% down payment rule is not a rule.

Yes, putting 20% down avoids PMI (private mortgage insurance). But FHA loans allow as little as 3% down, and there are good conventional programs at 5% and 10%. Don't delay buying for years saving for a number you don't actually need.

4. Get the home inspected. Always.

A good inspector in Tulsa costs $250-$350. The cheapest inspection I ever arranged found $14,000 worth of foundation work that the seller paid for. Don't skip this step to save a few hundred dollars on what may be the biggest purchase of your life.

5. Don't fall in love with the first house.

You should see at least 5-8 houses before writing an offer, even if the first one feels right. You need a baseline for what your dollar buys in your target neighborhood. (That said: in a hot market, do not wait so long that "the one" is gone.)

6. School district matters even if you don't have kids.

Resale. If you ever sell — and most people do within 7-10 years — the school zone is the single biggest factor in how fast your home moves and at what price. Edison, Jenks, Union, and Bixby consistently command a premium in our market.

7. Walk the neighborhood at three different times.

Saturday morning, Tuesday at 6 PM (rush hour), and Friday at 10 PM. You will learn more about your future neighborhood in those three visits than any listing sheet can tell you.

8. Read the seller's disclosure carefully.

In Oklahoma, sellers must complete a property condition disclosure. Read every line. If something is checked "unknown" that seems like it should be known (the age of the roof, for example), ask why.

9. Budget for the first six months.

You will need a lawn mower. A refrigerator (most don't stay). A washer-dryer. Curtains. A new mailbox lock. Set aside 1-2% of the purchase price for move-in expenses in addition to closing costs.

10. Use a buyer's agent — it doesn't cost you anything.

In a standard Oklahoma transaction, the seller pays the commission for both agents. Having your own representation costs you nothing and gives you someone whose only loyalty is to you. (Yes, this part is a little self-serving. But it is also true.)

Preparing Your Home to Sell

The five things that consistently give my sellers the biggest return for the least money:

  1. Paint. Neutral, light, fresh. The whole interior if you can afford it; trim and entryway if you can't. Paint is the single highest-ROI improvement in real estate.
  2. Curb appeal. Fresh mulch, edged lawn, weeded beds, clean front door, polished hardware. Buyers form an opinion in the first eight seconds.
  3. Declutter. Pack up half of what's on the shelves and in the closets. Buyers need to see the space, not your stuff.
  4. Clean. Professional carpet cleaning, professional window cleaning, and a deep kitchen and bath scrub. About $400 total. Pays back tenfold.
  5. Fix the obvious. The broken switch plate, the dripping faucet, the missing fence picket. Buyers count small flaws and assume there are big ones hiding.

Glossary of Real Estate Terms

TermWhat It Means
AmortizationThe way your loan is paid off over time. Early payments are mostly interest; later payments are mostly principal.
ARMAdjustable Rate Mortgage. Interest rate changes at set intervals. Lower introductory rate, more risk later.
Comp / CMA"Comparable" sales. A Comparative Market Analysis is the report that tells you what similar homes have sold for recently.
Earnest moneyA good-faith deposit you put down with your offer (typically 1% of price). Held in escrow; applied to closing costs.
EscrowA neutral third party that holds money and documents until both sides perform their obligations.
FHAFederal Housing Administration. Government-insured loan program with lower down-payment requirements.
PITIPrincipal, Interest, Taxes, Insurance. The four components of a typical monthly mortgage payment.
PMIPrivate Mortgage Insurance. Required on most conventional loans with less than 20% down. Falls off automatically once you reach 22% equity.
PointsPrepaid interest. One point = 1% of the loan amount, paid up front to lower your interest rate.
Title insuranceInsurance that protects you (and your lender) against problems with the chain of ownership of your home.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to buy a house?

From the day we start looking to the day you get the keys: typically 45-60 days. Most of that is the 30-day closing period after your offer is accepted. The lender will need every minute of it.

How much does a real estate agent cost?

For buyers, almost always nothing — the seller pays both agents out of the sale proceeds. For sellers, the standard commission in the Tulsa market is 6%, split between the listing and buyer's agents.

What is the Tulsa market doing right now?

(May 1996.) Healthy and balanced. Average days on market in the GTAR MLS is running about 52 days. Median sale price is up 3.4% year over year. Inventory is normal. It is a fine time to either buy or sell.

Do you handle commercial real estate?

Residential only. For commercial transactions I will happily refer you to two or three excellent commercial brokers I trust.

Do you work weekends?

Most weekends, yes — that is when most buyers can see houses. I do take Sunday mornings off for church, but you can leave a message on my pager any time and I will return it the same day.

Will you work with my out-of-state family member?

Absolutely. I have closed many relocation transactions remotely using videotape walkthroughs, fax, and overnight mail. I am also experimenting with sending photos by e-mail attachment — the technology is improving every month.
